Overview

Building an insurance
platform from the ground up

Aventum is a Lloyd's of London-backed insurtech operating at the intersection of complex risk and modern technology. When I joined, the product existed only as a vision — there was no design system, no user research, no validated flows, and no product on screen. My mandate was to design the entire SaaS platform: from concept to market-ready.

Insurance is one of the most opaque, jargon-heavy industries in the world. The challenge wasn't just designing screens — it was understanding a domain most designers never touch, translating it into intuitive digital workflows, and making enterprise software that insurance professionals actually wanted to use.

The Core Problem

Insurance underwriting and policy management is done in legacy systems — spreadsheets, emails, paper trails. Aventum needed a platform that digitised this entire workflow without disrupting the way specialist brokers and underwriters think. The product had to feel native to their world, not foreign.

Research Insights

What I discovered
in the field

Three weeks of shadowing underwriters revealed patterns no desk research could have surfaced. These insights became the design north stars for every decision that followed.

INS — 01
Context-switching kills productivity

Underwriters had 9–12 tabs open simultaneously to piece together a risk picture. Every screen switch cost cognitive load and introduced errors. The platform needed radical information consolidation.

INS — 02
Trust is built through data transparency

Users didn't trust black-box calculations. They needed to see the logic behind every premium figure, every risk flag. Showing the "why" behind data increased confidence and adoption significantly.

INS — 03
Jargon is a feature, not a bug

Early prototypes used plain language. Users rejected them — the domain terminology was part of their professional identity. The UI needed to honour the language of insurance, not oversimplify it.

INS — 04
Errors are expensive — prevention over recovery

A single data entry error in insurance can invalidate an entire policy. Users needed real-time validation, smart defaults, and inline guidance — not post-submission error states.

INS — 05
Collaboration is invisible in legacy tools

Multiple stakeholders — broker, underwriter, actuary — worked on the same risk sequentially without visibility into each other's work. Building shared state and audit trails was a critical unlock.

INS — 06
Speed matters more than aesthetics

Enterprise users prioritised task completion speed above all else. Beautiful UI meant nothing if it slowed them down. Every design decision was benchmarked against time-on-task metrics.

Design Decisions

The key calls
that shaped the product

Single-pane risk overview

Instead of multi-tab workflows, I designed a persistent risk panel that showed the complete context of any submission — coverage details, risk flags, documents, and comments — in one scrollable view. Eliminated the 9-tab problem instantly.

Progressive disclosure for complex forms

Insurance quote forms can have 80+ fields. I designed a stage-by-stage disclosure system that revealed fields only when relevant, reducing perceived complexity by 60% while capturing all required data.

Transparent calculation engine UI

Built an expandable "rate breakdown" component that showed users exactly how premiums were calculated — each factor, each multiplier. This single decision was the most-praised feature in user testing.

Shared state collaboration layer

Designed a real-time activity feed and task assignment system native to each submission. Brokers could see underwriter notes, actuaries could flag questions — all without leaving the submission context.

Domain-accurate design system

Built 90 components specifically for insurance workflows — risk tables, coverage toggles, premium sliders, document upload with parsing indicators. Every component was tested with real users before entering the library.

Smart validation with contextual guidance

Implemented inline validation that not only caught errors but explained why they occurred and what the correct value should be — pulling from insurance regulation data. Reduced support tickets by over 40%.

"The hardest part of designing for a specialist domain is learning to respect the complexity rather than simplify it away. Insurance professionals are expert users — your job is to match their mental model, not impose a new one."

Project Timeline

14 months,
zero to launch

Month 1–2
Domain Research & Stakeholder Alignment

Embedded with underwriting teams. Conducted 18 stakeholder interviews. Mapped existing workflows in Miro. Established design principles with product and engineering leadership.

Month 3–4
Information Architecture & System Design

Defined the 3-module structure (Quote, Policy, Claims). Built the IA for each module. Started the component library with atomic design principles. First wireframes validated with users.

Month 5–9
Core Product Design & Iteration

Hi-fi design of all core flows across 3 modules. 90-component design system completed. 4 rounds of usability testing. Bi-weekly design reviews with engineering. Major pivots on quote form and risk panel based on user feedback.

Month 10–12
Edge Cases, Polish & Dev Handoff

Designed 200+ edge case states — empty states, error states, loading states, permission states. Conducted design QA with engineering. Accessibility audit and WCAG 2.1 AA compliance.

Month 13–14
Beta, Launch & Post-Launch

Supported closed beta with 3 Lloyd's of London syndicates. Rapid iteration on beta feedback. GTM launch. Monitored analytics and session recordings post-launch. Awarded Customer Experience Champion.

Reflections

What this project
taught me

🧭
Domain expertise is a design superpower The weeks I spent learning insurance weren't "preparation" — they were design work. Every hour of research saved three hours of redesign. Invest in understanding the domain before opening Figma.
🤝
Engineering partnership matters more at scale A 0→1 product requires designers and engineers to be in daily conversation. Design decisions affect architecture. Architecture constrains design. The earlier you build that trust, the better the product.
🔬
Test with extreme users, not average ones Our best insights came from the most experienced underwriters — they found edge cases, questioned assumptions, and pushed us toward robust solutions. Intermediate users validate; experts stress-test.
🏗️
A design system is a product in itself Building the component library wasn't overhead — it was what allowed us to design consistently at speed across 3 modules. Treated it like a product: versioned, documented, and maintained.
