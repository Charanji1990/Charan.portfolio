Overview

Designing for
the most important click

BrightChamps is one of Asia's fastest-growing EdTech platforms — teaching Coding, Robotics, Financial Literacy, Gen AI, Math, English, and Science to kids aged 6–16 across India, Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, and the US. With millions in marketing spend driving traffic to landing pages every month, even a 1% conversion improvement meant significant revenue. The pressure was very real.

When I took on this project, the existing landing pages were built for volume — fast to ship, not optimised for conversion. The UX was generic, the messaging was broad, and the journey from landing to purchase had too many friction points. My mandate: redesign the high-performing pages, run structured A/B tests, and measurably improve conversion across all four key markets.

The Core Problem

Parents landing on BrightChamps pages were interested but unconvinced. The pages had decent traffic but low trust signals, unclear value propositions, and a checkout flow that created hesitation. Visitors were leaving at the moment of decision — not the moment of discovery.

Design Process

A data-first approach
to design decisions

01
Conversion Audit

Full funnel analysis using Hotjar, GA4, and session recordings. Identified drop-off points, rage clicks, and hesitation moments across all pages.

02
Parent Research

15 depth interviews with target parents (India, SEA, US). Uncovered their key anxieties, trust triggers, and the mental model they used to evaluate EdTech products for kids.

03
Hypothesis Framework

Built a prioritised hypothesis backlog using ICE scoring (Impact, Confidence, Ease). 22 hypotheses, narrowed to 12 A/B tests based on potential conversion impact.

04
Design & Test

Rapid variant design in Figma. Implemented via VWO. Each test ran for 2–3 weeks to statistical significance. Iterated winners into the base design.

05
Roll Out & Monitor

Winning variants baked into full page redesigns. Launched across 3 markets with localisation adaptations. Continuous monitoring for 8 weeks post-launch.

Research Insights

What parents
really needed to say yes

INS — 01
Trust is the #1 purchase driver

Parents weren't buying a course — they were trusting a company with their child's screen time. Peer reviews, instructor credentials, and real student outcomes mattered more than any feature list.

INS — 02
Price anxiety peaks at checkout

Heatmaps showed users scrolling to the price section multiple times before acting. The price wasn't too high — the perceived value wasn't clear enough to justify it confidently.

INS — 03
Kids are the real decision influencers

Parents often made the financial decision but kids validated it. Pages showing child-friendly content, fun learning visuals, and peer success stories saw higher "trial class" sign-ups.

INS — 04
Free trial CTA massively outperformed "Buy Now"

The commitment gap was too large for a first visit. "Book a Free Trial Class" reduced friction by letting parents experience before investing. This became the primary CTA across all pages.

INS — 05
Market-specific trust signals matter

Indian parents responded to "CBSE/ICSE curriculum aligned" and parent testimonials. SEA parents wanted "teacher credentials". US parents prioritised "cancel anytime" and money-back guarantees. One page couldn't serve all three.

INS — 06
Mobile UX was the real problem

62% of traffic was mobile. The existing pages were desktop-first designs crammed into smaller screens. Form fields were too small, CTAs were below the fold, and checkout had too many steps on mobile.

Design Decisions

How we redesigned
for conversion

Outcome-first hero section

Replaced generic illustrations with real student outcomes ("Aryan, 10, built his first app in 4 weeks"). Parents bought outcomes for their children, not features. Showing the destination drove scroll depth by 14%.

Progressive commitment model

Redesigned the entire funnel around a free trial class as the primary conversion goal. This reduced the commitment from ₹15,000 to zero, then nurtured enrolled trial users to paid. Dramatically improved top-of-funnel numbers.

Mobile-first form redesign

Converted the 3-field inline form into a step-by-step modal with progress indicators. Each step had one decision. Form completion on mobile jumped 31% — the single biggest conversion win of the project.

Trust architecture

Built a systematic "trust stack" — live class count, instructor credentials, parent video reviews, money-back guarantee, and curriculum badges — arranged by the sequence in which parents needed reassurance.

Market-specific page variants

Designed distinct hero messaging and trust signal hierarchies for India, SEA, and US. India page led with curriculum alignment; US page led with "cancel anytime" guarantee. Same product, different conversion triggers.

Price anchoring with value framing

Reframed pricing from "₹X/month" to "₹X per class — less than a pizza" with a breakdown of what's included. The absolute price didn't change. The perceived value did. Checkout rate improved by 9%.

"Conversion is not about tricks or urgency tactics. It's about understanding exactly where in the decision journey your user is stuck — and removing that specific obstacle. The rest is just noise."

Reflections

What conversion design
really taught me

📊
Data tells you where, not why Heatmaps and analytics pinpointed where people dropped off. But only interviews revealed why they left. Both are essential — neither is sufficient alone. Always pair quantitative signals with qualitative depth.
🎯
One metric, one test We failed early when we tested multiple changes at once. Isolating variables was critical — without it, you can't know what actually moved the needle. Discipline in test design is discipline in learning.
🌏
Localisation is not just translation Different markets had completely different trust hierarchies. A single global page was always going to underperform. Cultural context shapes conversion as much as UX mechanics do.
📱
Mobile is where conversions die or live Over 60% of traffic, under 40% of the design attention. Mobile-first isn't a trend — in EdTech targeting parents, it's the only way to think. Desktop is the exception, not the rule.
