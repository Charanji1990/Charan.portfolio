Overview

Leading design for
India's online meat market

Tendercuts is one of India's leading online fresh meat delivery platforms — delivering cleaned, cut, and packaged meat directly to customers' doors. Operating in a high-trust, high-sensitivity product category (fresh food, precise cuts, delivery timing), the UX stakes were uniquely high.

I joined as Lead UX Designer with full product ownership across all digital surfaces — the mobile app, web platform, and internal operations tooling. The platform was functional but showing friction: drop-offs in checkout, confusion in the browsing experience, and a product catalogue that was hard to navigate. My task was to diagnose the real issues and redesign the user flows end-to-end.

The Core Problem

Customers knew what they wanted to buy but couldn't easily get there. The browsing and checkout flow had accumulated years of quick fixes and feature additions — creating a fragmented experience full of dead ends and redundant steps. People were abandoning not because they didn't want the product, but because the path to checkout was too hard.

Design Process

From diagnosis
to full redesign

01
Flow Audit

Mapped every existing user flow across the app and web. Identified 14 friction points through session recordings, analytics funnel analysis, and customer support ticket review.

02
Customer Research

Conducted 20 intercept interviews with existing customers. Explored their mental model around buying fresh meat online — trust signals, cut preferences, delivery expectations, repeat behaviour.

03
Prioritisation

Scored all 14 friction points by conversion impact vs. engineering effort. Identified the 5 highest-leverage fixes — the ones that together could shift order volume substantially.

04
Redesign

Full hi-fi redesign of browse, product detail, cart, and checkout flows. Designed new category architecture, smarter product cards, and a streamlined 2-step checkout. Usability tested at every stage.

05
Ship & Measure

Staged rollout — browse first, then PDP, then checkout. Measured impact at each stage. Post-launch monitoring for 12 weeks with weekly analytics reviews and rapid iteration.

Research Insights

What customers
told us (and showed us)

INS — 01
Fresh meat is a high-trust purchase

Unlike most e-commerce, customers couldn't inspect the product before ordering. Visual freshness cues — photography, hygiene certifications, cut clarity — were the primary trust builders. Poor product imagery caused abandonment.

INS — 02
Cut selection caused the most confusion

Customers didn't know what "curry cut" vs. "biryani cut" vs. "boneless" meant visually. The product catalogue used text descriptions where images and visual comparisons were needed. This was the #1 reason for cart abandonment.

INS — 03
Repeat customers had a very different pattern

70% of orders came from repeat customers who knew exactly what they wanted. The browsing experience was designed for discovery — but the majority needed a "reorder" path. There was no shortcut for loyalty.

INS — 04
Delivery time was the #1 conversion factor

For fresh meat, delivery time wasn't a nice-to-have — it was essential information that determined whether customers ordered at all. This needed to be visible from the product card, not buried in checkout.

INS — 05
Checkout had 3 unnecessary steps

The existing checkout had 7 steps. Analysis showed steps 3, 5, and 6 could be eliminated or combined. Each eliminated step directly improved completion rate — a 43% reduction in steps yielded a 28% improvement in checkout completion.

INS — 06
Category navigation was broken

The browse experience grouped products in ways that made sense internally but not to customers. Chicken was split across 4 categories. Customers searching for "chicken" had to know which subcategory to look in. Reorganisation was overdue.

Design Decisions

The calls that drove
40% order growth

Visual cut guide on PDPs

Replaced text-only cut descriptions with a visual guide showing each cut option with a photo, weight range, and "best for" cooking suggestion. Time on PDP increased. Cut-selection abandonment dropped significantly.

Delivery time on product cards

Added "Delivered by 7 PM today" or "Delivered tomorrow" directly on product cards in the browse view. Customers no longer had to reach checkout to learn if delivery was viable for their meal plan. Browse-to-cart improved immediately.

Reorder tab for repeat customers

Pinned a "Reorder" shortcut to the home screen for returning users showing their last 5 orders. One tap to add previous order to cart. Repeat purchase frequency increased — the most profitable improvement in the entire project.

Side-cart drawer — no context switch

Replaced the "go to cart" full-page redirect with a side drawer that slid in while keeping the browse visible behind it. Customers could review and adjust without losing their browse position. Bounce from cart dropped by 22%.

Trust-first product cards

Redesigned product cards to lead with freshness signals: FSSAI badge, "Freshness Guaranteed" micro-label, and product photography that showed actual cut quality. Trust is not implied in fresh food — it must be designed in explicitly.

Customer-facing category architecture

Reorganised the full product catalogue from internal naming ("SKU-Chicken-Bone-In") to how customers actually thought ("Chicken · Bone-In Pieces · Curry Cuts"). This restructure alone reduced search usage by 38% — customers could browse without needing to search.

"When you're selling something as intimate as fresh food, every pixel is a trust signal. Design isn't decoration here — it's the difference between a customer who converts and one who goes to the supermarket instead."

Project Timeline

How the redesign
rolled out

Phase 1
Audit, Research & Prioritisation

6-week deep audit of all product surfaces. 20 customer interviews. Session recording analysis. Produced a prioritised friction map that became the redesign roadmap, signed off by product and business leadership.

Phase 2
Browse & Category Redesign

Restructured the full product catalogue. Redesigned browse cards with delivery time and trust signals. Launched to 20% of users first. Measured browse-to-cart uplift. Rolled out to 100% after 2 weeks of positive data.

Phase 3
Product Detail Page Overhaul

Rebuilt PDPs with visual cut guides, freshness badges, and nutritional info. Added side-cart drawer. Usability tested with 8 participants. Launched alongside browse redesign for cohesive experience.

Phase 4
Checkout & Reorder Redesign

The highest-stakes phase. 7-step checkout collapsed to 4. Reorder tab launched to all users. Extensive QA with engineering. 2-week phased rollout. This phase alone accounted for the majority of the +40% order increase.

Reflections

Leading design at scale —
what I took away

🎯
Friction is never one thing The 40% order increase didn't come from one big fix. It came from 14 small friction removals working together. No single redesign element could have done it alone. Systematic diagnosis beats hero solutions.
🔁
Design for your most loyal users first 70% of orders came from repeats. We were over-investing in first-time-buyer flows and under-investing in loyalty. The reorder tab was built in a week and had outsized business impact. Know your core user deeply.
🛡️
Trust is architecture, not decoration In high-sensitivity categories, trust signals need to be woven into every screen, not bolted onto a landing page. Customers make micro-trust decisions at every tap. Design that system explicitly or lose them silently.
📐
Full product ownership changes how you design Being responsible for the entire product — not just one feature — forced me to think in systems, not screens. Decisions in browse affected checkout. Checkout informed PDP. Everything was connected, and the design had to be too.
