Overview

Designing the face of
AI-powered retail security

Visu.ai is a UK-based AI retail security platform that uses computer vision to detect theft and fraud in brick-and-mortar stores — 24/7, in real time. The product integrates with existing CCTV and point-of-sale systems to monitor both customer behaviour and cashier activity, surfacing incidents the moment they happen and building a record of repeat offenders.

I was brought in as a UX consultant to work alongside the internal designer — guiding the full design of both the web platform and mobile app. The technology was powerful and genuinely innovative, but the product needed a design layer that made AI-driven security intelligence feel immediate, clear, and actionable for retail store owners and managers who are not technical users.

The Design Challenge

Retail owners don't think in model confidence scores — they think in alerts, incidents, and money. The platform had to translate complex AI detections into fast, decisive moments of clarity: something's happening, here's what it is, here's what to do. The speed and simplicity of that moment was the entire product experience.

Scope of Work

Web and mobile —
two surfaces, one story

The product needed to live on two platforms serving different contexts of use. I guided the designer through both, ensuring the experience was purposefully adapted to each surface rather than just scaled up or down.

Web Platform
Command centre for store managers

The web dashboard was designed for deeper engagement — reviewing flagged incidents, managing the Repeat Offender Watchlist, analysing cashier performance trends, and configuring alert thresholds. Built for desktop-first use during dedicated review sessions.

Mobile App
Real-time alerts in your pocket

The mobile app was designed around urgency — receiving instant push alerts when an incident is detected, reviewing live footage clips, and taking immediate action. Designed for on-floor use by managers who can't be at a desk when something happens.

Key Design Decisions

The calls that made
AI feel immediate

Alert-first mobile architecture

The mobile app was designed around the alert as the primary unit of experience. Every other feature — watchlists, history, settings — was subordinate. When a detection fires, the path from notification to viewing the incident clip had to be under 3 taps, every time.

Human-readable AI outputs

The AI detection engine outputs confidence scores and event classifications. We never exposed this raw data to users. Instead, every detection was translated into plain-language incident cards: what happened, where, when, and how to respond — no ML knowledge required.

Unified incident timeline

Rather than separate logs for Cashier Watch and Customer Watch, the web dashboard used a single chronological incident feed across both modules. Store owners could see their full security picture in one scroll — no toggling between sections to understand a shift.

Watchlist built for action, not just record

The Repeat Offender Watchlist was more than a database — it was designed as an action hub. Each offender entry showed their detection history, last seen location, associated incidents, and a one-tap option to export for police reporting. Built to be useful under pressure.

Onboarding through connection, not instruction

The setup flow got users to their first live detection as fast as possible. Rather than lengthy tutorials, we designed a guided camera connection flow where completing each step immediately showed a live preview — the reward was seeing the product work, not reading about it.

Progressive detail in incident cards

Incident cards were designed at two levels: a compact summary for scanning the feed quickly, and an expanded detail view for investigation. Store owners could triage 10 alerts in 30 seconds, then deep-dive only into the ones that warranted attention.

Design Insights

What designing for retail
security taught us

Understanding the real context of use — a store owner receiving an alert mid-shift while serving customers — fundamentally shaped every design decision we made.

INS — 01
Speed is the UX

A retail theft alert is time-critical. Every second between detection and awareness is a second the incident can escalate. The entire mobile experience was optimised around reducing the gap between "something happened" and "I know what to do."

INS — 02
Non-technical users need zero ambiguity

Store managers aren't security analysts. If an alert required interpretation, it had already failed. Every output from the AI — detection type, confidence, suggested action — had to be self-explanatory without any training or context.

INS — 03
False positives are product-killing

One false accusation in a retail context carries serious consequences. The UI needed to communicate confidence levels clearly, frame outputs as "suspicious activity detected" rather than accusations, and build in easy dismissal flows to protect users and their customers.

INS — 04
Context collapses on mobile

What works as a rich dashboard on desktop becomes overwhelming on a 6-inch screen mid-shift. The mobile app required radical prioritisation — only the most critical information in the alert moment, with depth available on demand but never blocking the core action.

INS — 05
Trust is earned through accuracy, not polish

Store owners quickly learned to trust or distrust the system based on whether its detections matched reality. The UX had to support that trust-building — making it easy to review, verify, and dismiss incidents so owners could calibrate their confidence over time.

INS — 06
Setup friction kills adoption

Camera and POS connection setup is unavoidably technical. Getting it wrong could mean the product never runs — and the owner never comes back. We invested heavily in making the setup flow guided, forgiving, and rewarding, so users reached their first live detection before they had a reason to give up.

"Designing for security means designing for high-stakes moments — when someone is mid-shift, under pressure, and needs the product to tell them exactly what's happening and exactly what to do. There's no room for ambiguity, and no margin for friction."

Reflections

What this project
sharpened in me

⚡
High-stakes UX demands a different standard When your product is detecting theft in real time, a confusing UI isn't just frustrating — it's a miss that costs money, or worse, leads to a wrongful accusation. Designing for genuinely high-stakes moments raised the bar for how precisely I think about clarity and hierarchy.
📱
Platform purpose must be decided before platform design The temptation with web + mobile is to design one and scale it. The better approach is to ask what job each platform is hired to do — and design from that intent. The web and mobile experiences for Visu.ai were fundamentally different products that happened to share the same data.
🤝
Consulting is about raising the ceiling, not filling the gap The internal designer didn't need someone to do the work for them — they needed someone to help them see further. The best sessions weren't when I gave answers; they were when I asked the question that made them arrive at a better answer themselves.
🎯
AI products live or die by the moment of output The AI can be technically brilliant. But if the moment it surfaces a detection doesn't feel immediate, clear, and trustworthy — the product fails. The design of that single moment — the alert, the incident card, the watchlist entry — is where the whole investment either pays off or doesn't.
